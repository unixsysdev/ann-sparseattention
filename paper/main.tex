\begin{abstract}
Sparse attention can reduce the cost of long-context transformer inference, but
the candidate-selection step remains a bottleneck. Exact top-$K$ attention over
native queries and keys preserves the most relevant keys but still requires
linear or quadratic scoring, while heuristic page-based methods such as Quest
scan context-length-dependent metadata. We study a post-hoc alternative:
training small per-layer query and key projections on a frozen language model so
that attention-relevant keys become nearest neighbors in a low-dimensional
retrieval space. This makes sparse attention compatible with off-the-shelf
approximate nearest-neighbor indexes such as FAISS HNSW~\citep{johnson2019billion,malkov2018hnsw},
without attention-aware graph construction.

On Qwen3-4B-Instruct-2507, training six attention layers with $d_{\mathrm{search}}=128$
adds 3.93M parameters, about 0.1\% of the base model. With block-causal packed
evaluation on WikiText-103, exact sparse substitution at $K=128$ is within
0.07\% relative perplexity of full attention, and FAISS HNSW matches exact
retrieval within 0.02 percentage points of relative perplexity. Compared with a
Quest-style page baseline, learned retrieval captures more teacher attention
mass at the same token budget, but paired NLL evaluation shows Quest is slightly
better at $K=128$ and statistically tied at $K=256$. The main contribution is
therefore not a perplexity win over Quest, but an ANN-native sparse-attention
design point: off-the-shelf indexing, asymptotically logarithmic candidate
scoring, and a dynamic-index mechanism validated by a suffix-retrieval proxy.
Under the same HNSW constants used in our FAISS experiments ($M=32$,
$\mathrm{ef}=64$), the operation-count crossover against Quest-style page
scoring occurs at roughly 298K tokens, with a 3.06$\times$ scoring advantage at
1M context.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}

Long-context transformer inference is increasingly limited by attention over a
large key-value cache. Sparse attention reduces the value aggregation workload
by attending to a subset of keys, but the system still needs to decide which
keys to load. This candidate-selection step determines whether sparse attention
is merely a quality approximation or a deployable long-context runtime.

The exact top-$K$ oracle computes scores against all keys and keeps the best
ones. It is a useful quality ceiling for top-$K$ sparse attention, but it does
not remove the scoring cost. Page-based methods such as Quest reduce this cost
by storing min/max metadata per page and scanning those metadata at inference
time~\citep{quest2024}. These methods are simple and strong at moderate context
lengths, but their candidate-scoring cost remains linear in the number of pages.

We explore a different tradeoff. Instead of searching over the model's native
queries and keys, we train a small retrieval space for each target attention
layer. The base model is frozen. For each trained layer, we learn
$W_Q^s,W_K^s$ so that $W_Q^s h_q$ retrieves the keys that the teacher attention
distribution assigns high probability to. Because both queries and keys are
trained into the same search space, standard ANN machinery can be used directly.
This addresses the practical issue identified by retrieval-based attention
systems: native Q/K vectors are not necessarily well behaved under vanilla
nearest-neighbor indexing~\citep{retrievalattention2024}.

Our current result is deliberately narrow. We do not show production wall-clock
speedups, GPU-resident ANN, or autoregressive KV-cache integration. We do show
that trained projections preserve full-attention perplexity on a clean
block-causal evaluation path, that FAISS HNSW is effectively lossless relative
to exact retrieval at $K=128$, and that the operation-count scaling becomes
favorable at very long context. We also include a proxy experiment showing that
suffix queries recover more teacher attention mass when older suffix keys are
available to the index, validating the mechanism behind dynamic insertion.

Our contributions are:
\begin{itemize}
    \item a post-hoc distillation method for training low-dimensional
    ANN-compatible retrieval projections on a frozen transformer;
    \item a clean block-causal evaluation showing full-attention parity on a
    six-layer Qwen3-4B pilot;
    \item a comparison to a Quest-style page baseline showing higher
    teacher-attention mass for learned retrieval but no current PPL advantage;
    \item an exact-vs-FAISS comparison showing that off-the-shelf HNSW preserves
    the learned retrieval result at $K=128$;
    \item an operation-count analysis and a dynamic-index proxy that identify
    the deployment regimes where learned retrieval may be preferable.
\end{itemize}

\section{Method}

\subsection{Search projections}

For each target attention layer $\ell$, we train two projection matrices
$W_{Q,\ell}^{s}, W_{K,\ell}^{s} \in \mathbb{R}^{d_{\mathrm{model}}\times d_{\mathrm{search}}}$.
Given hidden states $h_i$, the search vectors are
\begin{equation}
q_i^s = W_{Q,\ell}^{s} h_i,\qquad k_i^s = W_{K,\ell}^{s} h_i.
\end{equation}
At inference time, sparse attention retrieves keys using cosine similarity in
this search space, then computes ordinary attention over the retrieved native
keys and values. The base model parameters are never updated.

In the pilot experiments, $d_{\mathrm{search}}=128$ and the trained layers are
$[4,8,12,16,20,24]$ of Qwen3-4B-Instruct-2507. This adds 3.93M trainable
parameters.

\subsection{Training objective}

The teacher is the frozen full-attention model. For each target layer, we
capture hidden states entering self-attention and reconstruct teacher attention
weights from post-RoPE native $Q,K$ on the target layers. The projection module
is trained with two losses:
\begin{equation}
\mathcal{L} = \alpha \mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{InfoNCE}} +
\beta \mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{KL}}.
\end{equation}
The InfoNCE positives are teacher top-$K_{\mathrm{pos}}$ keys for each query.
The KL term aligns the search-space distribution with the teacher attention
distribution. In the reported pilot, $\alpha=\beta=1$, $K_{\mathrm{pos}}=16$,
and the model is trained for 1000 steps.

\subsection{Block-causal packed evaluation}

Short WikiText examples are packed into 4096-token sequences. A naive packed
attention mask allows tokens to attend across document boundaries, producing a
large leakage confound. We therefore assign every token a segment id, reset
position ids at segment boundaries, and construct a four-dimensional additive
mask that permits attention only when key and query belong to the same segment
and the key is causal. The same eligibility mask is used for teacher capture,
loss masking, retrieval metrics, and sparse-attention evaluation.

\section{Experiments}

\subsection{Setup}

All experiments use Qwen3-4B-Instruct-2507 and WikiText-103. Unless stated
otherwise, evaluation uses packed block-causal batches of length 4096. The
headline clean checkpoint is the $d_{\mathrm{search}}=128$ six-layer run at
step 1000. We report relative PPL gap against full attention:
\begin{equation}
\mathrm{gap} = \frac{\mathrm{PPL}_{\mathrm{sparse}} -
\mathrm{PPL}_{\mathrm{full}}}{\mathrm{PPL}_{\mathrm{full}}}.
\end{equation}
We also report mass@$K$, the teacher attention probability assigned to the
retrieved key set. This is a retrieval-fidelity metric and should not be read
as a direct substitute for PPL.

\subsection{Clean block-causal quality}

Table~\ref{tab:block-causal} shows the clean d128 result. The exact learned
retrieval path is effectively at full-attention parity.

The earlier packed-with-leakage run produced large negative PPL gaps. Those
numbers do not survive clean block-causal masking and should not be treated as
a denoising headline. The clean result supports the narrower claim that learned
retrieval can preserve full-attention quality on this slice.

\subsection{Quest-style page baseline}

We compare against a Quest-style min/max page selector with page size 16,
native post-RoPE Q/K, and the same block-causal token eligibility mask. This is
a correctness baseline, not an optimized Quest runtime. Table~\ref{tab:quest}
shows the aggregate results.

\begin{table}[h]

\label{tab:quest}

\midrule
Learned exact & 128 & 0.744 & 0.787 & 30.47 & +0.07\% \\

Learned retrieval captures more teacher attention mass at the same nominal
token budget. However, paired NLL evaluation gives a more conservative quality
comparison. At $K=128$, learned retrieval is slightly worse than Quest by
$0.00205$ NLL with 95\% bootstrap CI $[0.00160,0.00251]$, corresponding to
about +0.206\% relative PPL. At $K=256$, the methods are statistically tied
with NLL delta $-0.00005$ and CI $[-0.00029,0.00018]$. We therefore do not
claim a PPL advantage over Quest.

\subsection{Asymptotic scoring analysis}

Candidate scoring is the cost of deciding which keys to attend to before the
sparse softmax and value multiply. For one query at context length $N$, we use
the following operation-count proxy:
\begin{align}
\mathrm{Full}(N) &= N d_{\mathrm{head}} = 128N,\\
\mathrm{Quest}(N) &= \frac{N}{16}\cdot 2d_{\mathrm{head}} = 16N,\\
\mathrm{HNSW}(N) &= M\cdot \mathrm{ef}\cdot \log_2(N)\cdot d_{\mathrm{search}}.
\end{align}
Using the same HNSW constants as our FAISS experiments, $M=32$,
$\mathrm{ef}=64$, and $d_{\mathrm{search}}=128$, the Quest/HNSW crossover is
approximately 297,937 tokens. Table~\ref{tab:scaling} shows representative
points.

\begin{table}[h]
\centering

\label{tab:scaling}
\begin{tabular}{rrrr}

Context & Quest ops/query & Learned HNSW ops/query & Quest / learned \\
\midrule

256K & 4,096,000 & 4,709,623 & 0.87$\times$ \\
512K & 8,192,000 & 4,971,767 & 1.65$\times$ \\

This is only an analytic scoring proxy. It does not include GPU memory
behavior, index construction, cache layout, or sparse gather implementation.
It does show that the learned-retrieval design has a different scaling regime:
Quest is cheaper at moderate context under these conservative high-recall HNSW
constants, while learned HNSW becomes cheaper at very long context.

\subsection{Dynamic-index proxy}

The current inference wrapper is prefill-only and sets \texttt{use\_cache=False},
so we do not yet evaluate autoregressive insertion into a live KV cache. As a
mechanism check, we construct a suffix-retrieval proxy. Each clean eval sequence
is split into a 1024-token prefix and a decode-like suffix. The dynamic proxy
allows suffix queries to retrieve from all same-segment prior keys. The static
proxy allows suffix queries to retrieve from prefill keys plus a 256-token local
suffix window, but hides older suffix keys.

\begin{table}[h]
\centering
\caption{Dynamic-index proxy at $K=128$ over 8 eval batches.}
\label{tab:dynamic}
\begin{tabular}{lr}
\toprule
Setting & Teacher mass captured \\
\midrule
Dynamic proxy & 0.972 \\
Static proxy & 0.928 \\
Static teacher mass available & 0.954 \\
Dynamic - static & +0.044 \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{table}

The proxy shows that hiding older suffix keys costs measurable teacher-attention
mass. It validates the mechanism that decoded keys should remain retrievable
when added to the same learned search space. It does not show task accuracy,
decode latency, or real incremental insertion quality; those require cache
integration.

\section{Discussion}

The current evidence supports a specific design point. Learned retrieval does
not currently beat Quest on perplexity at $K=128$, and it ties Quest at
$K=256$. Its advantages are architectural: the retrieval space is directly
compatible with standard ANN, candidate scoring can be logarithmic in context
length, and new keys can in principle be inserted into the same index during
generation. These properties are most relevant at very long context and in
decode-heavy workloads, not necessarily in moderate-context prefill.

The results also show why paired evaluation matters. Aggregate PPL parity with
full attention can hide a small but statistically robust ordering between two
sparse methods. Likewise, retrieval mass is useful for diagnosing whether the
search space captures teacher attention, but higher mass does not guarantee
lower PPL.

\section{Limitations}

This is a prototype study. The main limitations are:
\begin{itemize}
    \item no production wall-clock benchmark; operation counts are analytic
    proxies;
    \item no GPU-resident ANN or fused sparse attention kernel;
    \item no autoregressive KV-cache integration; dynamic indexing is supported
    only by a retrieval-mass proxy;
    \item experiments are on one model family, one model size, one dataset, and
    six target layers;
    \item evaluation slices are modest, and multi-seed confidence intervals for
    training are not yet reported;
    \item Quest and FAISS are correctness baselines rather than optimized
    production implementations.
\end{itemize}

\section{Conclusion}

Training low-dimensional retrieval projections provides an ANN-compatible route
to sparse attention in frozen transformers. In a clean Qwen3-4B pilot, the
method preserves full-attention perplexity and allows FAISS HNSW to match exact
learned retrieval, while remaining competitive but not superior to a
Quest-style page baseline on PPL. The most promising argument for the method is
therefore not immediate quality improvement, but deployment structure:
standard ANN compatibility, favorable long-context scoring asymptotics, and a
natural path to dynamic index insertion. Turning that structure into a strong
systems result requires the next engineering step: GPU-resident retrieval and
decode-mode KV-cache integration.
